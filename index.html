Organizations in motion

Exploring organizations, their uses and challenges through creative coding exercises.

0. What is a model

Models can be thought of as many things. As a system of equations that takes an input and outputs a result. As a map. As a story.

We live only in the here and now, but models allow us, in a certain sense and for a limited number of issues, to be everywhere and everytime.

This is why we use models to predict what would happen, to measure the consequences of our decisions. We also use models to diagnose what went wrong.

There are brilliantly elegant models that rest on flawed assumptions of the real world. There are models that ask the wrong questions.

This is why a model is not a role-model and building it is in fact the first cybernetic act.

1. The need for an organization

Opportunity acquisition

Agents can share common goals. An organization offers its members the means to identify opportunities.

For this experiment the agents (the red dots) have been modeled as pseudo-random walkers The random walk is a process through which a mathematical space is traversed iteratively from the starting point, moving in each iteration with one unit on a particular dimension. This algorithm can be used to simulate brownian motion, the movement of amoebas on a surface, to trace a foraging animal or even to map stock prices. It this case the agents don't start at the origin point of the space and can move simultaneously on the X and Y direction. in pursuit of a destination (the green dot). Each agent only has a limited range of acquisition represented by the pulsing red circle. It will identify the opportunity only if it finds it in its range.

The actors are placed on grid with a random offset. The goal is randomly placed.

The model doesn't necessarily looks like what it models. You can think of this simulation in as literal a way as possible: an entity, be it an amoeba, a grazing cow, a drunk man or a submarine, searching for something in low visibility conditions. But this can simulate any kind of searching over an unstructured domain: looking for an arbitrary turn of phrase in a book of poems or finding the differences between nearly matching photos. In these cases the pixels would represent the words, respectively where the eye is falling on the images.

The agents in the first canvas (Fig. 1a) are pursuing the goal on their own. When reaching the destination they will simply stop. The same space is covered multiple times over. It can take a long time for all of them to reach their goal.

Fig. 1a

The agents in the second canvas (Fig. 1b) will communicate to each other the position of the goal. In this way, the agent to finish last will do so with only a small delay compared to the fastest one.

Fig. 1b

You can retry the experiment. There is even a slight possibility that all the independent actors in the first canvas will reach their destination before any in the second one.

Play with the parameters for the agents on each canvas. Notice how much fitter (faster and/or with a larger search radius) the agents exploring on their own have to be to reach the goal in a comparable time with those able to collaborate. Also note that the more collaborating agents there are, the quicker they all reach the destination.

Fundamental to this result are lines of communication, a shared goal and a command structure.

In this case the lines of communication have been presumed to have perfect integrity and considerable speed.

It's self-evident that the goal is shared.

The command structure is perfectly horizontal. Each member can issue to each other the full spectrum of possible tasks, which in this case consists of one single command.

This is a naive organizational model that we'll complicate later on.

Resource pooling

Actors are pursuing opportunities in according to their needs. When their needs aren't met after a period of time, they expire. As before, we can think of this from the literal and prosaic, such as animals dying from lack of food, to the more complex, such as employees quitting a job if they don't find the tasks they're given stimulating enough.

Fig. 2a

The actors will satisfy their needs by acting on task points.In this case the task points disappear after a period of time to simulate scarcity. The actors are also aware of the task points' position, they don't need to discover them of the board at this point. They will go to the nearest free task point and perform some kind of work on it, gaining the means to satisfy their needs, up to a limit. We can imagine this to be a solitary prey animal or a freelancer jumping from gig to gig.

Fig. 2b

This creates a very competitive environment. But what if the learn to share? If an actor has a surplus, it'll try to identify the nearest member of its organization lacking the resource it needs and impart to it half of what it acquired.

Fig. 3b

Even uncoordinated actors inside an organization tend to make better use of a scarce resource through sharing, instead of monopolizing it.

This allows for more complex behaviors. Let us make the reasonable assumption that actors cannot be active all the time.

Or that always immovable.

Skills transfer or knowledge reproduction

Let us now consider that actors naturally expire after a set time, even if their needs are met.

Fig. 3a

It isn't surprising what happens such actors are placed in an environment with no knowledge of its opportunities.

Fig. 3b

As long as the organization takes in new members at a higher rate than the expiration rate of the prior members and it allows the time for the acquired information to be diffused, no new member has to go through the discovery process. For the sake of clarity I've had the actors in this scene not share between them, instead focusing only on reaching the opportunity point and communicating. This is one way to build up institutional knowledge.

Fig. 3c

This kind of social spreading of information, where the group acts as a storage of knowledge isn't unique to humans. For example, there's a well known experiment of crows distributing among the flock information about researchers whom they perceive to be dangerous.

2. How organizations fail

Faulty communication

Up to this point we've modeled the communication between actors as lossless and consistent. There are many environments where this is not the case. Let us consider a situation where the identified opportunities are not stable, but instead expire. The actors will not only pursue expired opportunities that they previously encountered, but they'll distribute this poisoned knowledge to new members who'll themselves waste time checking for an expired opportunity.

Fig. 4a

There's also a symmetric phenomena, where the opportunities are stable but the transmitted information gets corrupted. Again for clarity's sake I've had the actors in this scene not share between them, instead focusing only on reaching the opportunity point and communicating. For the actor this might appear identical, arriving at what they presumed to be an opportunity and finding nothing actionable, but it's distinct enough to warrant a separate representation.

Especially considering that this kind of noisy communication can have multiple causes: itself a feature of the environment (a noisy medium or a medium poorly suited for the chosen communication channel), a poor communication protocol or poorly using the communication protocol. Demonstrating how each of these factors affect the communication between actors is outside the scope of this project, but I hope it can at least help you consider how they all factor in and especially how very different problems might create very similar problematic results.

Fig. 4b

Consider how this faulty communication turns the density of an organization from an asset into an issue that impedes not only on its growth, but on its very functioning.

For the first image the simulation was configured so that the actors were spawned with a low frequency, resulting in a sparse organization, with the wrong information naturally filtered out. In the second one the actors were spawned at a much higher rate, creating a dense organization that perpetuated wrong information.

Notice how in the canvas above the actors are clustered around the opportunity points. Despite some setback many of them arrive at the correct site and perform their task or satisfy their need. Whereas in this one they are more evenly distributed between the opportunity points, with the clusters encircling the points showing much less pronounced, hinting that they haven't reached their destination.
